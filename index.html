Intellectual Property (IP)

Intellectual Property (IP) is a term that describes the application of the mind to develop something new or original. IP can exist in various forms; a new invention, brand, design or artistic creation. There are various types of IP available.

Key concepts of IP protection

  • IP has many of the same ownership rights as physical property.
  • It is important that you effectively manage your IP to ensure you get the best protection and the most out of your idea/invention.
  • The creator of IP is not necessarily the owner - it is important that ownership is addressed through appropriate contractual arrangements.

    • Value of IP Rights

    IP rights are an incentive to encourage and reward innovation. They provide the owner with the opportunity to take advantage of their creation while limiting competition for varying periods and in various ways.
    Protecting and managing your IP is important when establishing your product or service in the market and is often the difference between success or failure. The existence of IP rights restricts competitive market forces for a set period.

    Copyright

    Copyright is probably the most common IP right. We create and encounter copyright daily. The creation of copyright is governed by the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1998 and provides for rights in the creation of literary, dramatic, musical, artistic, visual, audiovisual, broadcasts and electronic works.

    Copyright Infringement

    Copyright infringement occurs when someone uses, copies, shares, or distributes copyrighted material without permission from the copyright owner, and without a legal exception.

    What is a Privacy?

    Privacy may be defined as the claim of individuals, groups or institutions to determine when, how and to what extent information about them is communicated to others. Privacy is your right to control what happens with personal information about you. (Source: http://oznetlaw.org).
    Privacy for users means that the user's personal and sensitive information remains private, unless that user wishes to disclose it.

    Privacy Legislation

      The Federal Privacy Act

    The main Act covering information privacy in Australia at the time of writing was the Privacy Act 1988. The Privacy Act is a Commonwealth Act. The stated purpose is: An Act to make provision to protect the privacy of individuals and for related purposes.

      The Federal Privacy Principles

    The Privacy Act contains 10 privacy principles which apply to businesses which collect and use personal information. The Act makes non-compliance with any of these principles a breach of privacy.

    Privacy Policies

    National Privacy Principle five requires organisations to be open about the way they collect, store and use personal information. An organisation must be prepared to give information about its personal information handling practices to any one who asks for it (with some exceptions of course). This information is commonly called a 'privacy policy'.

    Business System and Continuity

    What is a critical business system?

    A system is critical for a commercial organisation if its failure results directly or indirectly in loss of life (for example, an air traffic control system) and/or major financial loss. When developing a disaster recovery plan (DRP) it is essential to identify critical systems and ensure they are restored as soon as possible.

    Identifying critical systems and data

    The business case or proposal for each new system should identify its importance and a risk analysis should be undertaken early in the project. This information may already be available in the project documentation, in which case you would review this material and identify the risk issues that have been raised.

    Impact of system failure

    When undertaking risk analysis and disaster planning, it is usual to focus on critical systems, software and data. The very definition of a critical system is that the business depends upon it and would be severely impacted if the system were not available.

    Risk Analysis

    Risk analysis is an analytical process undertaken to evaluate system assets and examine their susceptibility to threats. Through this process we evaluate the possible commercial losses that may result from the loss of these assets.

    Plan Testing and Maintanence

    Why test?

    • To make sure the plan works as expected
    • To find any weak points or missing steps
    • To train staff so they know what to do during a real incident

    Why maintain?

    • Businesses change: new systems, new risks, or new staff
    • Outdated plans can fail during a real disaster
